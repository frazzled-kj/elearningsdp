Part 2

Lesson: Anxiety & Trauma-Related Disorders

This is a short textbook-style mini-lesson on how to distinguish generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, acute stress disorder, and PTSD.

Overview: Stress and Anxiety Disorders

Stress is the process by which we perceive and respond to events we appraise as threatening or challenging. Anxiety disorders involve excessive fear or anxiety that is difficult to control and causes distress or impairment.

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) involves excessive anxiety and worry occurring more days than not for at least six months, about a number of events or activities. The worry is difficult to control and is often associated with restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, or sleep disturbance.

Panic Disorder involves recurrent, unexpected panic attacks, along with persistent concern or behavior change lasting at least one month after the attacks. Panic attacks are abrupt surges of intense fear that reach a peak within minutes and include physical symptoms such as heart palpitations or shortness of breath.

Acute Stress Disorder (ASD) involves symptoms that begin after a traumatic event and last from three days to one month. Symptoms can include intrusive memories, negative mood, dissociation, avoidance, and arousal.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) involves exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence, followed by intrusive symptoms, avoidance, negative changes in cognition and mood, and arousal changes. Duration is more than one month and symptoms cause functional impairment.

Worked Example 1

Maria was in a car accident two weeks ago. Since then, she has intrusive memories, difficulty sleeping, and irritability. Her symptoms began soon after the trauma and have lasted for under one month.

Analysis: Because these symptoms began after a traumatic event and have lasted less than one month, this pattern is consistent with acute stress disorder rather than PTSD.

Worked Example 2

For the past eight months, Amir has felt on edge most days. He worries about grades, his parents' health, friendships, and money. He reports muscle tension and difficulty sleeping, and says he “cannot turn off” the worry. There was no single traumatic event that started these symptoms.

Analysis: The long duration (more than six months), broad range of worries, and lack of a specific trauma point toward generalized anxiety disorder. The key cues are chronic, uncontrollable worry about many domains and physical symptoms such as muscle tension and sleep problems.

Worked Example 3

Over the past month, Jordan has had several sudden episodes of intense fear that peak within minutes. During these episodes, Jordan feels short of breath, dizzy, and fears losing control. The episodes occur unexpectedly, and Jordan now avoids crowded spaces and worries about having another attack.

Analysis: The abrupt, unexpected surges of fear, plus ongoing fear of future attacks and avoidance, match the pattern of panic disorder more than GAD or trauma-related disorders.

Guided Lesson: Practicing Diagnostic Cues

In this version of the lesson, you will learn by working through guided and partially guided cases that highlight key diagnostic cues like duration, triggers, symptom patterns, and functional impairment.

Key Disorders and Cues (Reference Table)

Disorder Trigger Duration Core pattern / quick cue
GAD No single trauma; worry about many everyday areas At least 6 months, most days Broad, uncontrollable worry plus physical symptoms
Panic Disorder Often no clear trigger; unexpected panic attacks Recurrent attacks plus at least 1 month concern or behavior change Sudden fear surges and fear of future attacks
Acute Stress Disorder (ASD) Clear trauma (accident, assault, disaster) 3 days to 1 month after trauma Intrusions, avoidance, negative mood, arousal soon after trauma
PTSD Clear trauma More than 1 month after trauma Intrusions, avoidance, negative mood, arousal plus functional impairment

Practice Quiz

Case 1

For the last six months, Daniel has experienced persistent worry about academic deadlines, relationships, finances, and future plans. He reports being on edge most days, with muscle tension and restlessness. The worry is difficult for him to control.
